Jenny Lind

Jenny Lind, born Johanna Maria Lind on October 6, 1820, in Stockholm, Sweden, was one of the most acclaimed opera singers of the 19th century. She was renowned for her soprano voice, often referred to as the "Swedish Nightingale." Lind's talent was recognized at a young age, and she received singing lessons from her first teacher, Erik Erikson. At age nine, Lind entered the Royal Dramatic Theatre's acting school in Stockholm. She made her public debut at 10 years old in the role of Agathe in Weber's opera "Der Freischütz." In 1841, Lind made her breakthrough in Germany, performing the role of Alice in Meyerbeer's opera "Robert le Diable" in Berlin. This performance catapulted her to fame across Europe. Lind's reputation spread to England, where she performed at Her Majesty's Theatre in London in 1847. She toured extensively in England and the United States, where she debuted in 1850.

Aside from her vocal talent, Lind was known for her philanthropy and humanitarian efforts. She frequently performed charity concerts, donating huge amounts of money to various causes, including hospitals and schools. Her generosity earned her the admiration and affection of many, and she was often referred to as "The Swedish Nightingale with a Heart of Gold."

Lind retired from the opera stage in 1852, at the height of her success, to devote her time to concert performances and philanthropy. She married Otto Goldschmidt, a German pianist and conductor, in 1852, and settled in England. Despite her retirement from opera, Lind continued performing concerts and volunteering for charity throughout her life. She died on November 2, 1887, in Malvern, Worcestershire, England, leaving behind a legacy as one of the best sopranos of the nineteenth century and a beloved humanitarian.

Lindomania

Barnum prepared a lot for Jenny Lind's American tour was a success by creating a publication blitz campaign to ensure Lind's name was spread across all publication papers. Her name and image were embossed, engraved, and embroidered on hats, bonnets, gums, soaps, cigars, etc.

Despite the huge success of Jenny Lind's grand arrival to America, which we know as Lindomania, Lind found herself in the midst of political chaos. The two topics—the debates over slavery and the arrival of Jenny Lind— dominated the newspapers in the year 1850. During that time, Congress passed the Compromise of 1850, which is a collection of laws that permits new territories to permit slavery and officials were given the power to recapture freed African Americans. These two topics fought front-page news. This explains why Barnum had to organize full-on marketing and advertising for Lind's tour and also why he had writers participating in a contest. The competition was aimed to grasp the mood of the public which was conveyed by the writers' submitted works.

Patriotism and Religion

Lind's American performances strengthened the nation's patriotic tendencies during the political chaos brought upon by the Civil War. Author and critic Walt Whitman wrote in the New York Evening Post that he was not impressed by Lind's performance and called her “Swedish Swan,” compared to the usual “Swedish Nightingale,” because he disdained her embodiment of Christian values. Jenny Lind’s voice and performances have always been described as pure and magnificent. Most people who attended her concerts described her performances as a “transcendence into the divine” and a “secular ritual of spiritual transformation.” Lind's Bird Song was marketed to the public with an image of a brown nightingale at the top with its wings spread apart and its beak pointed towards the skies (heaven). The position is normally a white dove representing the Holy Spirit in Christianity.

Songwriting Competitions

Lind's American tour had a significant impact on the formation of America's national identity heavily based on white Christian middle-class values. Lind's Swedish/European background marked her as a symbol of the “Old Country.” Her Swedish ancestry made her palatable to the public, which was only a generation away from the Revolutionary War against the English. Lind performed Scottish and Irish ballads, and even a Norwegian song called Echo Song, which invokes the picture of a pastoral shepherdess, to please new white immigrants. Lind's visit became a catalyst for the formation of American society with the sole image of white Christian values in an era of political unrest and disagreements about slavery.

Barnum would conduct competitions wherein songwriters, composers, and poets could submit a self-written song for Lind to be sung in one of her performances. The winning poem was titled 'Greeting to America' which was about being welcomed by the land of the free and that the country should remain united as one so peace could shine light on the banner of stars (the stars on the American flag represent the states).

Disappointed Abolitionists

A New England quartet called Hutchinson Family Singers, who were abolitionists, visited Lind after her arrival. Jesse Hutchinson wrote a song titled “Welcome to Jenny Lind” with lyrics that praise Lind while also giving the impression of wanting Lind to back up their antislavery cause. They sang, “From the snow-clad hills of Sweden, like a bird of love from Eden, Lo! She comes with songs of freedom, Jenny comes from o'er the sea.”

However, Jenny Lind ignored the implications by praising their performance. Even to this day, people are yet to know Lind’s stance on slavery despite her public image of being charitable, pure, and kind. Her disinterest in the topic of slavery led to disappointment from the abolitionists. Abolitionists wanted her to demonstrate her “support” (if she had one) to the antislavery cause by extending her philanthropy to abolitionist organizations. There was a rumor that Lind donated a thousand dollars to an antislavery cause, which Barnum quickly denied for fear of alienating the pro-slavery South.
